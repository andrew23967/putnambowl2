Strategy 1 — Pick underdogs X% of the time

The simplest possible rule: for every game, flip a biased coin — pick the underdog with probability X%, otherwise pick the favorite. The chart sweeps X from 0% (always favorite) to 100% (always underdog) and shows expected season score. The shaded band is the P10–P90 range of actual season outcomes; the error bars on the mean show the 95% confidence interval on the estimate itself. If the best strategy's error bars clearly don't overlap with 0% or 100%, there's a real sweet spot.

Strategy 2 — Only pick underdogs with large enough payouts

A smarter rule: pick the underdog only when the point value is above a threshold. Each bar shows the net expected value of picking the underdog over the favorite for games in that point range. Green = pick the underdog. Red = pick the favorite. Error bars are 95% confidence intervals — if a bar doesn't clearly clear zero, the result could be noise. With {{ ev_results|length }} buckets tested, you'd expect roughly {{ ev_results|length }} × 5% ≈ 1 false positive by chance, so look for bars well clear of zero.

Strategy 3 — Always pick certain teams

Are some teams consistently better picks than others? Each bar shows the net expected value of always picking that team over their opponent, across all their games in the selected seasons.

Important — multiple comparisons problem: With {{ team_ev|length }} teams tested at 5% significance, you'd expect about {{ team_ev|length }} × 5% ≈ 1–2 teams to appear "significant" purely by chance. The standard ±CI bars account for each team individually. The ★ Bonferroni marker is the stricter threshold — it corrects for all {{ team_ev|length }} simultaneous comparisons, so a team needs a much larger effect to earn it. Only Bonferroni-starred teams are reliably good or bad picks.
