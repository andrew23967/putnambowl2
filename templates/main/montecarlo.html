PutnamBowl Analysis

Can a simple strategy
beat PutnamBowl?

In PutnamBowl, picking the underdog earns more points than picking the favorite — but underdogs win less often. The system is designed so that favorites and underdogs are, in theory, roughly equal expected value. But in practice, some patterns in real NFL outcomes might tip the scales.

This page tests three simple strategies using historical NFL data going back to 2016: always picking underdogs at a fixed rate, picking underdogs only above a point threshold, and always picking certain teams. Strategy 1

Pick underdogs X% of the time

The simplest possible rule: for every game, flip a biased coin — pick the underdog with probability X%, otherwise pick the favorite. There is no intelligence to this; you are not looking at matchups, teams, or lines. You are just dialing in a fixed preference.

The simulation runs this strategy across all selected seasons {{ config.n_trials }} times each, computing your total season score each time. The chart below shows the expected season score for each value of X. The shaded band covers the 10th–90th percentile range of actual season outcomes, showing how much variance you should expect. The error bars on the mean line are the 95% confidence interval on the mean estimate itself — they tell you how precisely the simulation has pinned down the true expected value.

Strategy 2

Pick underdogs only when the payout is large enough

A smarter version of Strategy 1: instead of blindly picking underdogs at a fixed rate, only pick the underdog when the point value is above some threshold. The idea is that small underdogs may not be worth the risk, but when the payout is high enough, the expected value tips in their favor.

Each bar represents a bucket of games grouped by the underdog's point value. The bar height shows the net expected value of picking the underdog over the favorite in those games, computed from historical outcomes. Green bars mean the underdog was the better pick. Red bars mean the favorite was better. Error bars show 95% confidence intervals. A bar that doesn't clearly clear zero is likely noise.

Strategy 3

Always pick certain teams

The final strategy: ignore the odds entirely and just always pick the same teams. Some franchises are consistently good; maybe always backing the Chiefs or fading the Jets has historically been the right call regardless of the line.

Each bar shows the net expected value per game of always picking that team over whoever they are playing. Teams are sorted from best to worst. Error bars are 95% confidence intervals on that estimate.

The catch: with {{ team_ev|length }} teams being tested simultaneously, you would expect roughly {{ team_ev|length }} × 5% ≈ 1–2 teams to appear "significant" by pure chance, even if no team is actually a better pick than any other. The standard ±CI bars account for each team individually — they are too lenient in this context. The ★ Bonferroni threshold corrects for all {{ team_ev|length }} comparisons at once. Verdict

No simple strategy beats PutnamBowl

Across all three approaches — picking underdogs at a fixed rate, targeting underdogs by payout size, and always backing certain teams — the evidence points in the same direction: no simple rule reliably outperforms random picking once you account for the noise in the data. The scoring system does a good job of balancing risk and reward, and the NFL's unpredictability takes care of the rest.

That said, the strategies tested here are deliberately naive. They ignore the game situation, the week of the season, how the teams have been performing, the size of the home field advantage, weather, injuries, and dozens of other signals that serious bettors spend years learning to use. A smarter strategy almost certainly exists — it just isn't a one-line rule.

Finding it is the whole point of PutnamBowl. The data is here. The simulation framework is here. It's up to you to find a better one.
